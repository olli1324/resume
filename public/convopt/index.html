▎ Why this plan is built around recognition

The failure mode is concept confusion, not missing knowledge

You have seen all 15 lectures already. Re-watching them is the slow path. The two previous attempts did not fall short on time or on guessing penalties — they fell short because similar concepts blurred together under pressure: LP vs QP vs SOCP, convex set vs convex function, primal vs dual, gradient vs Newton, KKT vs Lagrangian.

So the work here is disambiguation, not re-reading. Each topic page leads with the one-line tell that separates it from its neighbours. The quiz drills classification under time. The flashcards hammer the confused pairs. Re-derivation is reserved for the few things that actually need it — KKT, the dual of a small QP, an SVD by hand.

The five-week schedule

Roughly 5–10 hours a week, ~35–50 hours total. Diagnose first, then disambiguate, then time yourself on past papers.

Week 1

May 13–19 · diagnose & reset
Find out where the confusion actually lives. Don't restudy what you already know.
  • Day 1 (~1.5h): take the diagnostic below under timed conditions. Score it honestly. Tag each missed question with its topic.
  • Day 2 (~1h): reread the cheatsheet end-to-end. Mark every line you can't immediately justify.
  • Day 3–4 (~3h): rebuild the linear-algebra bedrock from L2–L4. Focus only on: gradient and Hessian of $\tfrac12 x^\top Q x + r^\top x$, SVD definition, Rayleigh quotient, projection onto a subspace, when a matrix is PSD.
  • Day 5 (~1.5h): practice 1 and practice 2 fully by hand. Check against the solution PDFs.

Week 2

May 20–26 · convexity & standard forms
Classify any problem (LP / QP / SOCP / GP / SDP) within 30 seconds.
  • Day 1 (~1.5h): reread L6 (convexity) and L7 (standard form). Build the disambiguation table from memory before checking it.
  • Day 2 (~1.5h): L8 (QP) and practice 4. Watch for QPs in disguise.
  • Day 3 (~1.5h): L9 (LP) and practice 5. Drill the $\ell_1$ vs Chebyshev reformulations — they get confused on exams.
  • Day 4 (~1h): L10 (SOCP) and practice 6.
  • Day 5 (~1h): mixed-classification drill. Write 10 problems on paper and classify each as LP / QP / QCQP / SOCP / SDP / GP / none.

Week 3

May 27 – Jun 2 · duality, GP, SDP
Write the Lagrangian and the dual for a small LP and a small QP without thinking.
  • Day 1 (~1.5h): reread L12 (duality). Re-derive the dual of $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \leq b$. Then the dual of $\min \tfrac12 x^\top Q x + c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax = b$. Don't peek.
  • Day 2 (~1.5h): practice 7 and 8. KKT on small examples.
  • Day 3 (~1.5h): L11 (GP). Posynomial recognition: which of $2x+3y$, $x^{-1}+y$, $-x+y$, $x^{0.5}y^2$ is a posynomial?
  • Day 4 (~1.5h): SDP and LMI from the Stanford slides. Schur complement is the one trick that does most of the work.
  • Day 5 (~1h): practice 9 and 9bis.

Week 4

Jun 3–9 · algorithms & mixed practice

The skill inventory — what the exam actually tests, and which session drills each

Pulled from the Calafiore & El Ghaoui exercise book and all 13 weekly practices. Ten areas, each with the skills and the specific session to drill them.

1. Linear algebra and geometry

You need to: write a word problem in standard form; project a point onto a line, hyperplane, affine subspace, or column space of $A$ ($A(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top p$); compute the distance from a point to a hyperplane $|a^\top x - b|/\lVert a\rVert_2$; gradients and Hessians of $\tfrac12 x^\top Q x + r^\top x$, $\lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2$, log-sum-exp, $\log\det X$; recognize subspaces, half-spaces, polyhedra, ellipsoids, simplices, norm balls.

Drill with: Practice 1 (thruster), Practice 2, Practice 3. Skip if the diagnostic showed no foundation gaps.

2. Symmetric matrices, eigenvalues, SVD

You need to: diagonalize a 2x2 or 3x3 symmetric matrix by hand; identify PSD via eigenvalues, leading principal minors, or $x^\top A x \geq 0$; apply Rayleigh ($\lambda_{\max} = \max_{\lVert x\rVert=1} x^\top A x$); compute the SVD and read off rank, range, nullspace, condition number, pseudoinverse; use $\log\det X^{-1} = -\sum\log\lambda_i$.

Drill with: Practice 4, Practice 5.

3. Least squares

You need to: solve $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2$ via $A^\top A x = A^\top b$ and say when the solution is unique; write the minimum-norm solution $x^\star = A^\top(AA^\top)^{-1}b$; set up Tikhonov / ridge; spot LS hiding inside a different-looking problem (weighted, constrained, total).

Drill with: Practice 6, exercise book chapter 6. (The minimum-norm formula is diagnostic question 5.)

4. Convexity recognition

You need to: prove convexity from the chord definition; use the Hessian PSD test; know the operations that preserve convexity (nonneg sum, max, affine composition, perspective, partial minimization); identify convex sets (sublevel sets, half-space intersections, second-order cones, PSD cone).

Drill with: Practice 7 (squared norm, log-sum-exp, Young's inequality).

5. Standard-form classification — the highest-leverage skill

Given a problem on paper, decide in under a minute: LP, QP, QCQP, SOCP, SDP, GP, or non-convex.

  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_\infty$ → LP, scalar auxiliary $t$.
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_1$ → LP, vector auxiliary $t$.
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2$ → SOCP (unsquared norm).
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2$ → QP or LS.
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2 + \lambda \lVert x\rVert_1$ → QP (Lasso shape).
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2 + \lambda \lVert x\rVert_2^2$ → QP (ridge).
  • $\min \lVert Ax-b\rVert_2^2 + \lambda \lVert x\rVert_1^2$ → not a QP; separate handling.

For SOCP specifically: squaring $c^\top x \geq \lVert x\rVert_2$ is not equivalent (the squared version is non-convex). Cast robust sphere-enclosure, facility-location, and portfolio-with-variance constraints as SOCP.

Drill with: Practice 8 exercise 1 (the four-function table) — the single most exam-predictive exercise in your folder. Then Practice 10 (SOCP) and Practice 9 (LP-shape reformulations).

6. Duality and KKT

You need to: write the Lagrangian; derive the dual $g(\lambda,\nu) = \inf_x L$; three canonical ones cold: $\min \tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2$ s.t. $Ax=b$, $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax\leq b$, $\min \lVert x-p\rVert^2$ s.t. $x \geq 0, \mathbf{1}^\top x = 1$; state and use the four KKT conditions; interpret a dual variable as a shadow price; know when strong duality holds (convex + Slater, LP whenever either side is feasible).

Drill with: Practice 11 — all three exercises. The LP sensitivity one is the one most likely to trip up "concept confusion".

7. Geometric programming

You need to: tell a monomial from a posynomial (positive coefficients only; any real exponents allowed; first minus sign breaks it); convert a GP via $y_i = \log x_i$; spot GP applications (positive variables, products, ratios).

Drill with: Exercise book chapter 9. No dedicated GP practice session beyond L11.

8. Semidefinite programming and LMIs

You need to: recognize an LMI $F(x) = F_0 + \sum x_i F_i \succeq 0$; apply the Schur complement to convert quadratic constraints into LMIs; cast eigenvalue min/max as an SDP.

Drill with: Exercise book chapter 11. Schur complement appears in multiple-choice as "is this convex?".

9. Algorithms

The exam asks conceptual questions about algorithms more than numerical ones. Practice 12 question 1 is the canonical example.

You need to: for gradient descent, identify a descent direction ($v^\top \nabla f < 0$), state the rates ($O(1/k)$ convex L-smooth, linear $1 - m/L$ strongly convex), and the descent-lemma step $t \leq 1/L$; for Newton, write $-(\nabla^2 f)^{-1}\nabla f$, affine invariance, quadratic local convergence; for Nesterov, $O(1/k^2)$ at the same per-iteration cost; for barrier, $-\sum\log(-g_i)$, central path, phase 1; for projected gradient, $x_{k+1} = P_C(x_k - t_k\nabla f)$ and projection onto box, ball, simplex, half-space.

Drill with: Practice 12 question 1 (true/false in exam format). Practice 13 for Nesterov and barrier.

10. Applications

The exam wraps the core math in an application skin. Undress them:

  • Markowitz portfolio: QP, or SOCP with a standard-deviation constraint.
  • SVM (soft-margin hinge): QP.
  • Facility location with Euclidean distances: SOCP.
  • Chebyshev approximation: LP via the scalar-$t$ trick.
  • Regression: LS, ridge, or Lasso depending on regularizer.
  • Robust LS with stochastic $A$: take the expected value, get a QP (Practice 8 exercise 2).

Drill with: Practice 10 ex 4 (portfolio), Practice 9 ex 3–4 (LP applications), exercise book chapters 13–16.

Exam-day tactics

The marking is +1 / −⅓ / 0 over roughly 11–12 multiple-choice questions. A blind random guess on 4 options has EV $(1/4)(+1) + (3/4)(-1/3) = 0$ — it breaks even, so never guess blind. But eliminate even one option and EV becomes $(1/3)(+1) + (2/3)(-1/3) = +1/9$ per question. Answer those.

  • About 4–5 minutes per question. If you've spent 7 on one, flag it and move on.
  • If you finish early, do not change answers on instinct. Only change if you find a concrete mistake — first-instinct corrections usually hurt.
  • Bring: scientific calculator, the cheatsheet (paper notes are allowed), the disambiguation table.
  • The night before: no studying after 8 pm. Sleep matters more than three extra hours of review.
The diagnostic — 10 questions, 45 min, no notes

Time yourself, no calculator at first. Tag each miss with its topic from the disambiguation table — that tagging is more useful than the score itself.

  1. Which of these sets is convex? (a) $\{x \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x_1^2 + x_2^2 \leq 1\}$, (b) $\{x : x_1^2 + x_2^2 \geq 1\}$, (c) $\{x : x_1 x_2 \geq 1, x \geq 0\}$, (d) $\{x : x_1^2 + x_2^2 = 1\}$.
    Answer. (a) only. (b) is the complement of a disk — non-convex. (c) is above a hyperbola in the positive orthant — non-convex (the segment between $(1,1)$ and $(100,0.01)$ exits the set). (d) is a circle, not a disk — non-convex.
  2. Which of these functions is convex on its domain? (a) $f(x)=x^3$ on $\mathbb{R}$, (b) $f(x)=x^3$ on $\mathbb{R}_+$, (c) $f(x)=\log x$ on $\mathbb{R}_+$, (d) $f(x)=\max(x, x^2)$ on $\mathbb{R}$.
    Answer. (b) and (d). (a) is concave for $x < 0$. (c) is concave. (d) is the max of two convex functions, hence convex.
  3. Classify in the tightest class (LP / QP / QCQP / SOCP / SDP / GP / non-convex): $\min x_1+x_2$ s.t. $\lVert(x_1,x_2)\rVert_2 \leq x_3$, $x_3 \leq 5$.
    Answer. SOCP. The constraint is exactly norm $\leq$ affine. Not an LP (norm is nonlinear), not a QP (constraints aren't quadratic in standard QP form).
  4. Classify: $\min x_1^2 + 2x_1 x_2 + 3x_2^2$ s.t. $x_1+x_2=1$. Convex?
    Answer. QP. $Q = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$; eigenvalues $2 \pm \sqrt{2}$ are both positive, so $Q \succ 0$ and the problem is convex.
  5. Write the KKT conditions for $\min \tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2$ s.t. $Ax = b$. Solve them.
    Answer. $L = \tfrac12\lVert x\rVert^2 + \lambda^\top(Ax-b)$. Stationarity: $x + A^\top\lambda = 0$, so $x = -A^\top\lambda$. Feasibility: $Ax = b \Rightarrow -AA^\top \lambda = b \Rightarrow \lambda = -(AA^\top)^{-1}b$. So $x^\star = A^\top(AA^\top)^{-1}b$ — the minimum-norm solution.
  6. Compute $\nabla f$ and $\nabla^2 f$ for $f(x) = \tfrac12 x^\top Q x - b^\top x + c$ with $Q \succeq 0$.
    Answer. $\nabla f = Qx - b$. $\nabla^2 f = Q$.
  7. Let $A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$. Find eigenvalues and the SVD.
    Answer. Eigenvalues 4 and 2 (trace 6, det 8, $\lambda^2-6\lambda+8=0$). Eigenvectors $(1,1)/\sqrt2$ for $\lambda=4$, $(1,-1)/\sqrt2$ for $\lambda=2$. For symmetric PSD: $U = V = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$, $\Sigma = \mathrm{diag}(4,2)$.
  8. Write the Lagrange dual of $\min c^\top x$ s.t. $Ax \leq b$.
    Answer. Standard form: $\max -b^\top\lambda$ s.t. $A^\top\lambda + c = 0$, $\lambda \geq 0$. Sign conventions vary between textbooks — what matters is that you can derive it.
  9. Is $f(x,y) = 2x + 3y^2 + xy^{-1}$ (with $x, y > 0$) a posynomial? Why?
    Answer. Yes. A posynomial is a sum of monomials with positive coefficients over positive variables. Every term here qualifies, including $xy^{-1}$ (negative exponents are fine; only negative coefficients break the structure). If the first term were $-2x$ it wouldn't be a posynomial.
  10. You're running gradient descent on a strongly convex $L$-smooth function with parameter $m$. Why pick $t = 1/L$ rather than $t = 1/m$?
    Answer. $t = 1/L$ is what the descent lemma needs to guarantee $f(x_{k+1}) \leq f(x_k) - (1/2L)\lVert\nabla f\rVert^2$. With $t = 1/m$ (larger than $1/L$) the step is too big and you can overshoot. The condition number $\kappa = L/m$ governs the rate $(1 - m/L)^k$.

Scoring

  • 5–7: Standard starting point. Follow the schedule as written.
  • 0–4: More serious gaps. Spend Week 1 on L6–L7 (convexity) and the cheatsheet, then start the schedule from Week 2 with a slower pace.
