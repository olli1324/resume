§1Conceptual core

Likely as MCQ + first open-ended question.

L1 · Energy transitions through history

Five transitions: wood → coal (steam engine, 1760s industrial revolution), coal → oil (internal combustion, 1900s), oil → gas (post-WWII), and currently fossil → renewables. Each transition took 40–60 years. The driver is always efficiency gain + new use cases, not pure cost.

Tell: energy density increases with each transition (wood ≈ 16 MJ/kg, coal ≈ 24, oil ≈ 42, gas ≈ 50, uranium ≈ 80,000,000). Solar/wind are not denser; the transition is driven by climate, not density.

L2 · The energy trilemma

Three competing goals that any energy policy must balance:

  • Sustainability — environmental impact, lifecycle emissions, land use.
  • Affordability — cost to consumers, fuel poverty, industrial competitiveness.
  • Security — supply reliability, geopolitical exposure, fuel-mix diversity.
Exam tell
Any two corners are achievable easily, three is the challenge. Norway gets sustainability + affordability (hydro), pays a security cost. Germany gets sustainability + (partial) security, pays affordability (Energiewende cost).

L3 · The need for transition + sustainability concept

Sustainability = Brundtland 1987: meeting present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs. Three pillars: environmental, economic, social. Climate driver: 1.5°C Paris target requires net-zero CO2 by 2050 globally; EU Green Deal 55 % by 2030.

L4 · Multi-layer dimensions

Five layers Bompard uses for any country analysis:

  1. Physical — resources, geography, grid.
  2. Technical — mix, efficiencies, capacity factors.
  3. Economic — prices, markets, investment.
  4. Political/regulatory — laws, targets, subsidies.
  5. Social — acceptance, equity, behaviour.

L5 · Modelling approaches

Three families used in the course:

  • Top-down (macroeconomic): GDP ↔ energy ↔ emissions elasticities.
  • Bottom-up (technology-rich): MARKAL, TIMES, individual plants.
  • Hybrid: WEM (World Energy Model), POLES, PRIMES.

Tell: top-down for elasticities, bottom-up for technology choice, hybrid for full national scenarios.

L6 · The electricity triangle

The strategy for decarbonising the energy system rests on three legs:

  1. RES generation — replace fossil power.
  2. T&D infrastructure — transmission and distribution adapted for variable supply.
  3. Electrification of demand — EVs, heat pumps, electric industry.
All three must move together: more RES without grid ⇒ curtailment; electrification without RES ⇒ emissions just shifted.

§2Electricity economics

Likely as open-ended #1 or #2.

L7 · Economics of power systems — key concepts

Power systems differ from other goods: supply must equal demand instantaneously. Three cost components:

  • CAPEX — upfront investment, depreciated over plant lifetime.
  • OPEX — annual O&M, taxes, insurance.
  • Fuel — variable with output.

Power-plant types ranked by CAPEX (low → high): OCGT < CCGT < coal < nuclear < offshore wind < PV utility. By marginal fuel cost: nuclear < wind/PV (zero) < coal < CCGT < OCGT.

L8 · Peculiarities of electricity as a commodity

  1. Storage is expensive — instantaneous balance required.
  2. Demand is largely inelastic in the short run.
  3. Transport via grid is constrained by physical laws (Kirchhoff).
  4. Network externalities — outages spread.
  5. Capacity must cover peak, not average.
  6. Significant lead times for investment (5–10 yr).
  7. Strong political and regulatory involvement.

L9 · Microeconomics review

Demand curve: marginal willingness to pay, downward-sloping. Supply curve: marginal cost, upward-sloping (in competitive market). Market equilibrium where they meet. Consumer surplus = area above price, below demand. Producer surplus = area below price, above supply.

Long run: all costs variable, firms enter until economic profit = 0. Short run: fixed costs sunk, firms operate as long as price > average variable cost.

Lerner index $L = (P-MC)/P$ measures market power. HHI = $\sum s_i^2$ where $s_i$ is market share in %. HHI < 1500 = competitive, 1500–2500 = moderately concentrated, > 2500 = highly concentrated.

L11 · Bidding and market clearing

Two main auction designs:

  • Uniform price (most EU spot markets): all accepted bids paid the clearing price = bid of the marginal accepted unit. Encourages bidding at true marginal cost.
  • Pay-as-bid: each accepted bid paid its own bid. Encourages strategic bidding (guess the clearing price).
Bompard's favourite question
Which design gives lower expected prices? — Trick answer: under perfect competition they give the same expected price. Pay-as-bid is not cheaper in equilibrium; bidders just shift their bids upward to chase the marginal-clearing price.

L12 · Classification of electricity markets

Time horizons:

  • Forward / futures — months/years ahead, financial hedging.
  • Day-ahead (DAM) — spot, hourly, closes 12–24 h before delivery.
  • Intraday (IDM) — continuous trading until close-to-real-time.
  • Balancing (BM) — system operator dispatches real-time reserves.
  • Capacity market — pays for availability, not energy.

§3Formulas

Likely the open-ended exercise. Memorise as a chain.

L13 · KKT conditions (the most important exercise)

For the optimisation problem:

$$\min_{x} f(x) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad g_i(x) \leq 0, \quad h_j(x) = 0$$

The Lagrangian is:

$$\mathcal{L}(x, \mu, \lambda) = f(x) + \sum_i \mu_i g_i(x) + \sum_j \lambda_j h_j(x)$$

The 4 KKT conditions (memorise these names + statements):

  1. Stationarity: $\nabla_x \mathcal{L} = 0$
  2. Primal feasibility: $g_i(x^*) \leq 0$ and $h_j(x^*) = 0$
  3. Dual feasibility: $\mu_i \geq 0$
  4. Complementary slackness: $\mu_i \cdot g_i(x^*) = 0$ for every $i$
Interpretation: $\lambda_j$ and $\mu_i$ are shadow prices — the marginal value of relaxing the corresponding constraint by one unit. In electricity dispatch the energy-balance $\lambda$ is the system marginal price.

Generation dispatch (canonical exercise)

For $n$ thermal generators with cost $C_i(p_i) = a_i p_i^2 + b_i p_i + c_i$, demand $D$:

$$\min \sum_i C_i(p_i) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_i p_i = D, \quad p_i^{\min} \leq p_i \leq p_i^{\max}$$

Equal marginal cost rule (for unconstrained generators):

$$2 a_i p_i + b_i = \lambda \quad \forall i \text{ in the interior}$$

Solve for $p_i = (\lambda - b_i)/(2 a_i)$, then plug into $\sum p_i = D$ to find $\lambda$, the system marginal price.

Shannon diversification index

$$H = -\sum_{i} s_i \ln s_i$$

where $s_i$ is the share of source $i$ in TPES. $H = 0$ means one source (no diversification); $H = \ln n$ means equal shares. Norway: low H on power side (hydro dominates), moderate H on TPES.

LCOE / NPV / IRR (full version in §5)

$$\text{LCOE} = \frac{\sum_k (I_k + O_k + F_k)/(1+r)^k}{\sum_k E_k/(1+r)^k}$$

$I_k$ investment, $O_k$ O&M, $F_k$ fuel, $E_k$ energy in year $k$. Compare to expected market price.

Energy and carbon intensity

Energy intensity (of GDP): TPES/GDP, MJ/€. Carbon intensity (of TPES): CO2/TPES, tCO2/TJ. Carbon intensity of GDP: CO2/GDP. EU mean ≈ 0.10 kgCO2/€; Norway about 0.04.

Unit conversions you must know cold

  • 1 Mtoe = 41.868 PJ = 11.63 TWh
  • 1 TWh = 3.6 PJ
  • 1 kWh = 3.6 MJ
  • 1 GWh thermal ≈ 0.085–0.6 GWh electric depending on efficiency

§4Oral answers

Each topic, memorise three lines: what it is, why it matters, the trap.

Trilemma
Three competing energy-policy goals (sustainability, affordability, security). Matters because every choice trades one against another. Trap: people forget security — e.g. shutting nuclear in Germany hurt security even as it helped sustainability.
Electricity triangle
RES + grid + electrification, the three legs of decarbonisation. Matters because moving only one fails. Trap: confusing it with the trilemma — this is strategy, that is constraints.
LCOE
Discounted total cost per discounted unit of energy. Matters because it lets you compare a 30-year nuclear plant with a 25-year solar farm. Trap: it does not capture system value (firm vs variable).
Uniform vs pay-as-bid
Uniform pays marginal price to everyone accepted; pay-as-bid pays each their bid. Matters because EU uses uniform. Trap: expected prices are the same under perfect competition.
Capacity factor
CF = actual energy / max possible energy. Matters because it's the bridge from installed MW to TWh. Trap: PV ~12–25 %, onshore wind ~25–40 %, offshore wind ~40–55 %, nuclear ~85–92 %.
KKT
Necessary conditions for a constrained optimum (stationarity, primal feasibility, dual feasibility, complementary slackness). Matters because all electricity-market clearing reduces to KKT. Trap: dual feasibility only applies to inequality constraints; equality multipliers are unsigned.
HHI
Sum of squared market shares in percent. Matters as the standard market-concentration metric. Trap: many people forget to square or to convert shares to percent — HHI > 2500 is a high-concentration alarm.
NPV vs IRR
NPV is absolute value at given discount rate; IRR is the rate making NPV zero. Matters because they can rank projects differently. Trap: IRR can be non-unique with sign-changing cash flows.
Shannon index
$-\Sigma s_i \ln s_i$ — entropy of the mix. Matters for supply-security analysis. Trap: it ignores correlations and political risk; high $H$ + all-Russian-gas is still bad.

§5Financial metrics (L16/L17) — almost guaranteed exercise

Time value of money

Future value: $F = P(1+r)^n$. Present value: $P = F/(1+r)^n$. Annuity (uniform yearly payment $A$ equivalent to present value $P$ over $n$ years):

$$A = P \cdot \frac{r(1+r)^n}{(1+r)^n - 1}$$

Inverse:

$$P = A \cdot \frac{1-(1+r)^{-n}}{r}$$

Fisher relation: $1 + r_{\text{real}} = (1+r_{\text{nom}})/(1+\pi)$, where $\pi$ is inflation. Plant discount rate ≈ real rate + risk premium (typically 2–5 %).

Power-plant parameters

  • CAPEX: construction cost in €/kW
  • OPEX: annual fixed O&M, €/kW/yr
  • Working hours: $T = \text{CF} \cdot 8760$ h/yr
  • Annual energy: $E = P_{\max} \cdot T$
  • Lifetime: PV 25–40, wind 20, CCGT 25–30, nuclear 60

Cash flows and taxes

Net cash flow in year $k$: $f_k = f_k^+ - f_k^-$ (revenues minus outflows). With tax rate $x$ and depreciation $D_k$:

$$T_k = (R_k - C_k - D_k) \cdot x$$

§6Cost functions (E1 pattern)

Heat rate → variable cost

Thermal plant heat rate: $H(p) = a p^2 + b p + c$ in Gcal/h, with $p$ in MW. Variable cost (with fuel price $f$ in €/Gcal):

$$C^v(p) = f \cdot H(p) = f a p^2 + f b p + f c \quad [\text{\euro/h}]$$

Investment cost → hourly equivalent

With overnight CAPEX $I_w$ (€/kW), size $P_{\max}$ (MW), availability $\alpha$, lifetime $L$ years:

$$t = 8760 \cdot \alpha \cdot L \quad [\text{operating hours}]$$
$$C^f = I_w \cdot P_{\max} \cdot 1000 / t \quad [\text{\euro/h, sunk}]$$

Total, marginal, average

Total cost: $C(p) = C^v(p) + C^f$. Marginal cost: $C^m(p) = dC/dp = 2 a' p + b'$, where primed coefficients are the variable-cost ones (so $a' = fa$, $b' = fb$). Average total: $\bar{C}(p) = C(p)/p$.

Worked example (E1)

Given $H(p) = 0.001837 p^2 + 2.2949 p + 11.2423$, $f = 19.6$ €/Gcal, $P_{\max} = 60$ MW, $\alpha = 0.93$, $L = 30$ yr, $I_w = 400$ €/kW:

  • $C^v(p) = 0.036 p^2 + 44.98 p + 220.35$ €/h
  • $t = 8760 \cdot 0.93 \cdot 30 = 244,404$ h
  • $C^f = 400 \cdot 60{,}000 / 244{,}404 = 98.2$ €/h
  • $C(p) = 0.036 p^2 + 44.98 p + 318.55$ €/h
  • $C^m(p) = 0.072 p + 44.98$ €/MWh (47.14 at 30 MW → 49.30 at 60 MW)
  • $C(60) = 3146.95$ €/h, so unit cost $\approx 52.45$ €/MWh

Sunk vs non-sunk in bidding

Short-run rule: operate if revenue covers variable cost. Long-run rule: enter the industry if revenue covers total cost. The $C^f$ term is sunk once built, but in the long run it's also avoidable.

§7Last-4-hours checklist

  1. One hour on KKT + dispatch — write out the Lagrangian + 4 KKT conditions on paper without looking. Then solve the worked example in §3 from memory.
  2. 30 minutes on financial metrics — write NPV, IRR, PI from memory. Reconstruct the CCGT vs TG comparison in §5. Know that NPV depends on size, PI on capital efficiency.
  3. 30 minutes on cost functions (E1) — write $H(p) \to C^v(p) \to C(p) \to C^m(p)$ chain. Distinguish sunk from non-sunk costs.
  4. 30 minutes on uniform vs pay-as-bid — sketch the merit-order diagram, label the clearing point, explain who gets paid what.
  5. 30 minutes on LCOE — write out the formula, name each term, do a quick numerical example.
  6. 30 minutes on the trilemma + multi-layer — sketch the triangle, list the five dimensions, give one example per dimension.
  7. 30 minutes on commodity peculiarities + market structures — list the seven peculiarities of electricity, name the four structures.
  8. 30 minutes on Shannon + CF + unit conversion — memorise Mtoe↔TJ↔TWh and the CF and Shannon formulas.

High-probability exam patterns (from E1–E5)

  • Pattern A (E5.1): future-value or annuity. One formula, one plug.
  • Pattern B (E5.4): payback year of a power-plant investment. Build cumulative cash-flow table; find year cumulative ≥ 0. Sometimes asks for the price needed so payback hits exactly 15 yr.
  • Pattern C (E5.5): compare two plants (TG vs CCGT) via NPV, IRR, PI. Annuity factor; rank by NPV and PI.
  • Pattern D (E1): derive variable cost from heat rate; add hourly equivalent investment cost; produce total, marginal, average cost; compute breakeven price.
  • Pattern E (E2/E3): market clearing with capacity or network limits. Lagrangian → KKT → marginal generator → shadow price.
  • Pattern F (E4): uniform vs pay-as-bid revenue, given merit-order stack and demand level.
